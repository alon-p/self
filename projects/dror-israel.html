Internal Systems for a Large Nonprofit

Building back-office systems from scratch for a large organization — including a print-ordering system with a self-service pricing engine that's still in daily use years later.

Stack
Python/TurboGears 2 · MariaDB · AngularJS · jQuery · Docker · AWS (Fargate / ECS)
Role
In-house full-stack developer
Context
In-house development for a large nonprofit organization

Overview

For several years I was the in-house developer building the internal systems a large nonprofit relied on day to day. I built these from scratch and ran the infrastructure they lived on, managing the organization's cloud resources on AWS. The systems included a meeting-room booking tool, a physical-archive management system, and the most interesting of the three: a print-house ordering system with dynamic pricing.

Featured: Print-House Ordering with Dynamic Pricing

The Challenge

Ordering prints across the organization was informal and manual. People emailed back and forth about what they needed printed, and prices were worked out through ad-hoc discussion. There was no consistent way to ask for a print job, and no consistent way to price one. With members across a large organization, that meant repeated questions and inconsistent answers.

The hard part technically: the print shop needed to add new products themselves, over time, without calling a developer every time. Pricing depended on many factors that varied per product — so I couldn't just hardcode a price formula per item.

The Approach & Key Technical Decision

Rather than build a fixed pricing formula, I built a dynamic pricing engine driven by configuration the staff write themselves. Each product is defined by a set of questions and possible answers, and I wrote a simple regex-based parser that lets staff express how the price should be calculated from those questions and answers — directly, without code.

It was a bit complex under the hood, but it achieved the key goal: the system stays fully dynamic. The print shop can add new products and define their pricing rules on their own, with no developer in the loop. I also formalized the whole intake into forms — every question a print job needs answered is captured up front, instead of being teased out over email.

Results & Impact

  • Replaced manual email threads and price haggling with a single, structured ordering system.
  • Formalized print requests into forms, so jobs come in complete and consistent.
  • The print shop can add and price new products without a developer, thanks to the self-service pricing rules.
  • It became the place people go to understand how to ask for prints — most of the answers they need are already in the system.
  • It's still in use today, years later, quietly helping people across a large organization get their printing done. That longevity is the part I'm proudest of.

Also Built

  • Cloud infrastructure — I managed the organization's AWS resources, running these systems on Docker with AWS Fargate / ECS.
